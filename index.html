Olympic Speedskaters to Face an Old Nemesis in Sochi: Air

When long track olympic speed skaters hit the ice this winter, they'll not only be competing against each other, but also trying to overcome the dense atmosphere found in low-altitude places like Sochi.

For many athletes, high altitudes mean worse performance. There's less air to breathe and less oxygen available for muscles. But speed skaters tend to perform better at high altitudes.

At high altitudes there's less air and therefore less drag on skaters' bodies as they race around the track. This makes them a few extra seconds or miliseconds faster--often enough to set a new olympic record. In fact, almost all of the records in individual olympic long track skating were set back in 2002, when the olympic skating track was at a record high of 4330 feet.

The track at Sochi this year is only at about 100 feet above sea level. Athletes hoping to break current olympic records will have to work extra hard to overcome the slowing effects of air resistance. But if any records are set, it will be especially impressive.
